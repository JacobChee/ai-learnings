## The problem

We had one good lead, and it came inbound. An EHS manager at Becton Dickinson — a global medtech manufacturer with a large plant in Tuas — reached out, interested in our ergonomic risk-assessment programme. His head office had told him to establish a baseline. Perfect fit for what AnjouHealth does, and we didn't have to chase it.

One lead is not a pipeline. The obvious next question — "who else in Singapore looks exactly like this?" — is a week of someone googling competitors, digging through LinkedIn, and guessing at email addresses. Nobody had that week.

## The move that worked: reverse the brief

The instinct is to ask AI to "do market research" or "find us customers". That produces a generic list you could've gotten from a directory.

I did the opposite. I gave it the **one lead as the specification** and asked it to reverse-engineer the profile: regulated manufacturer, real production lines with manual handling, a dedicated EHS function, and a head-office mandate driving the timing. Then: find every company in SEA/APAC that matches.

That reframing is the whole trick. The lead already encodes what a good customer looks like. You don't need the AI to invent an ideal-customer profile — you need it to pattern-match against one you've already validated with money and interest.

## What it built

A tiered list of **28 companies** across four industries — medtech (Medtronic, Abbott, Thermo Fisher), pharma (GSK, Pfizer, Novartis, Amgen, Sanofi), semiconductor (Micron, GlobalFoundries, STMicroelectronics) and industrial/FMCG (P&G, 3M, ExxonMobil) — each scored A/B/C on how closely it matches the Becton Dickinson template, with the specific Singapore/Malaysia sites, the ergonomic risk rationale, and the right job titles to target.

It also grounded the "why now" with real numbers it pulled and checked: work-related musculoskeletal disorders are Singapore's leading occupational disease at roughly **60% of confirmed cases**, manufacturing is the top contributing sector, there were **353 confirmed WRMSD cases in 2023**, and MOM has been intensifying ergonomic inspections. That's the regulatory pressure that makes the head-office mandate urgent.

## The part that actually unlocked it

Here's the counter-intuitive bit. The market research wasn't the hard part — and the tool I expected to do the work didn't.

Plain web search is US-region and surfaced **zero** named Singapore EHS managers. It's fine for "which pharma companies have Tuas plants", useless for "who runs safety at the Tuas plant".

What worked was letting the agent drive **my own logged-in LinkedIn session** through the browser, one company at a time. That found real, named EHS owners: an EHS Manager at GSK, a Manager of EHS at Thermo Fisher's Pharma Services Group, an EHS Senior Engineer at Micron, two EHS Managers at STMicroelectronics. It even flagged that one of them was already a **first-degree connection of mine** — a warm intro hiding in plain sight.

## What didn't work (the honest limits)

This is not a magic "send" button. Real tradeoffs:

- **It finds names and email *patterns*, not verified addresses.** Six companies have publicly confirmed formats (Becton Dickinson, Medtronic, Abbott, Thermo Fisher, Micron, GSK — and our own lead's address confirmed the BD pattern). The rest are inferred `first.last@domain`. About half the contact list is tagged "confirm before sending". The agent was clear about which, instead of pretending.
- **A free LinkedIn account throttles search.** I got clean results for about ten target companies before risking the limit, so the contact column has deliberate gaps. It's not all 28.
- **Out-of-network profiles hide surnames.** The Abbott EHS manager showed as "Kelvin L." — enough to find, not enough to build an email without a confirming click.
- **One company (Novartis) returned no clearly-labelled EHS person** on the first pass. The agent said so rather than inventing one — which I'd rather have.

I told it explicitly: don't fabricate names, and don't scrape — browse what I can already see in my own session. That boundary is why I'm comfortable with the output.

## The tailored pitch

Once the targets and contacts existed, generating the actual pitch was the cheap part. It wrote per-company proposals — addressed to the named EHS manager, built around that company's specific operations (cleanroom and aseptic handling for GSK; high-throughput packaging and warehousing for Thermo Fisher), opening with the head-office-baseline trend our real lead represents, and mapping each risk to our RULA/REBA assessment.

![Tailored GSK pitch, name redacted](/results/one-lead-to-pipeline/pitch-gsk.png)

I asked it to do these **two at a time, highest email-confidence first**, and stop until I'd reviewed each pair. That pacing matters — it keeps me reading every doc before it's sent, instead of trusting a batch of 28.

## And the email that carries it

A pitch PDF still needs a covering email. It drafted one per lead — short, addressed to the named EHS manager, leading with the same head-office-baseline angle and closing with a low-friction ask. Here's the GSK one (name and address redacted):

```text
Subject: An ergonomic risk baseline for GSK's Singapore sites

Hi [name],

I lead workplace ergonomics at AnjouHealth (part of Physio & Sole
Clinic Group). We're currently running a baseline ergonomic risk
assessment for a leading medical-device manufacturer at its Singapore
plant — a programme their head office mandated.

GSK's Singapore operations face the same brief, especially with MOM
stepping up ergonomic inspections and WRMSDs now Singapore's leading
occupational disease. Our assessment uses motion analysis with
RULA/REBA scoring, is MOM-aligned, and is led by physiotherapists.
A short proposal tailored to your operations is attached.

Open to a 20-minute call? I'd also offer a no-cost pilot on one
high-risk line so your team can see the methodology first.

Best regards,
Jacob Chee
AnjouHealth — Physio & Sole Clinic Group
```

And here it is loaded into our webmail — the tailored pitch and our ergonomics deck already attached, ready to send (recipient redacted):

![Outreach email composed in webmail, recipient redacted](/results/one-lead-to-pipeline/email-gsk.png)

The catch ties straight back to the limits above: the agent writes the email, but it can't confirm the address. Every one gets verified by hand first. The AI drafts — it doesn't hit send.

## What it cost

**$0.** This ran in Cowork on **Claude Opus 4.8** (desktop, with file and browser tools), inside the subscription we already pay for — no metered API billing, no per-token charge for the session. The only real input was a couple of hours of an afternoon, most of it me reviewing the output.

The comparison isn't "AI vs. a researcher". It's "this pipeline exists vs. it never gets built because nobody had the week".

## What I'd do next

1. **Verify the inferred emails before any send** — start with the Tier A targets, confirm each surname and address via the profile or a verification tool.
2. **Close Becton Dickinson first**, then ask permission to use it as a named reference — the social proof is what makes the cold outreach to its look-alikes warm.
3. **Run the remaining LinkedIn lookups** (Stryker, J&J MedTech, Amgen, Roche, the EMS players) to fill the contact gaps left by the free-account throttle.
4. **Use the first-degree connections as intros** instead of cold-emailing — one of them already sits inside the target set.
5. **Keep generating the pitches two at a time**, highest confidence first, reviewing each before it leaves my outbox.
