## The problem

A friend sells men's cotton pyjama pants on a Singapore marketplace. Seven plaid
designs, decent margins, and exactly one piece of creative: a flat product listing
image. No video, no budget for a creator, no time to shoot.

The conventional move is to brief a UGC creator (S$200–500 a clip here), wait a week,
and hope they read the brief. I wanted to know if an AI pipeline could skip all of that
and produce something you'd actually let run as an ad.

## What I actually did

I ran a Higgsfield "content factory" agent. The whole thing is button-driven — no
prompt engineering on my side. The flow:

1. Upload the single product photo.
2. The agent registers it as a "product" and reads the design off the image.
3. Pick a format and a creator (a preset avatar), and it writes the full shot script
   itself.
4. It renders a 9:16, 1080p clip with synced audio and a talking voiceover.

I rendered two clips:

- **A "comfiest-pants test"** — a guy attempts a backflip, fails, lands on the bed, and
  deadpans straight into a cotton-and-waistband review. 12 seconds.
- **An honest features walkthrough** — different guy, the camera "bumps" his forehead,
  he recovers and walks through the drawstring, the soft cotton, the straight-leg cut.
  15 seconds.

Both are bedroom-set, handheld, and — this is the part that got me — genuinely pass as
real phone footage.

## Where the creative comes from

This is the part I almost undersold. The agent doesn't invent random skits. It works from
a library of UGC formats that are **already winning in the product's category** — short
challenge clips, sidewalk-style stranger interviews, premium unboxings, honest talking-
head reviews, audio-first ASMR close-ups. These aren't generic; they're the shapes that
actually trend for loungewear and cheap-comfort apparel right now. The system's job is to
map *your* product into a proven format, not to guess at one.

My two clips are the two highest-fit formats for this product: a comfort "challenge" and
an honest review. So the creative direction isn't coming from me having a good idea at
11pm — it's coming from formats that are demonstrably pulling views in this exact
category.

One honest caveat: I jumped straight to the build step this run, so I leaned on that
format library rather than kicking off a fresh live trend-scrape across TikTok/Reels/
Shorts first. The full pipeline does that research pass up front; I skipped it because the
category formats here are well-established and I wanted output fast.

## The third clip nearly sold me

The seller then generated one more — a female creator in one plaid, holding up another
pair: a *"my girlfriend just ordered another colour"* social-proof beat. Different proven
format (social proof / haul), same flow, same product photo. It's the most convincing of
the three, and it's the one I'd never have thought to script.

## The counter-intuitive bit

I assumed the believability would come from image quality. It didn't. It came from the
**format choice plus the engineered mistakes**.

The two hooks I picked are canned templates: *Epic Fail* (botched backflip) and
*Camera Bump* (operator clips the subject's head). They're deliberately clumsy. And that
clumsiness — baked into formats that already go viral — is what makes the clip read as a
real person filming themselves rather than an ad. A clean, polished render looks like an
ad and gets scrolled past. The flaw is the format, and the format is the point.

So the lever isn't "make it look more real." It's "pick a viral format and let it look
like an accident."

## What didn't work

Honest tradeoffs, not self-inflicted ones:

- **Faces drift unless you pin them.** If you don't lock a specific preset avatar, the
  model casts a fresh random face on every render. Across a campaign that's death — your
  "creator" changes identity clip to clip. You have to explicitly pin an avatar.
- **It invented a hangtag.** The first render put a paper swing-tag on the front of the
  pants — because the source photo was a retail listing shot. I had to explicitly say
  *no hangtag, no swing tag, clean fabric only* in the negative prompt to remove it. The
  model copies what's in your reference image a little too faithfully.
- **One reshoot, straight away.** My first take cast a creator I didn't want. Re-rendering
  is cheap but not free — an extra 120 credits, costed below.
- **The 15-second ceiling is real.** Every clip is capped at 4–15s. Anything longer has to
  be designed as two clips and stitched outside the tool.
- **Getting the files out takes a manual step.** The finished videos live on a CDN my
  tooling couldn't reach, so pulling the mp4s (and stills) meant grabbing them from the
  player by hand.

## The reusable part

There's no clever script — the agent writes the prompt itself. The one thing worth
keeping is the generation call shape, because the two non-obvious fields (`mode` and a
pinned `avatar`) are what keep a campaign consistent:

```json
{
  "model": "marketing_studio_video",
  "mode": "Product Review",
  "product_ids": ["<your-registered-product>"],
  "hook_id": "<camera-bump-template>",
  "setting_id": "<bedroom>",
  "avatars": [{ "id": "<pinned-preset-avatar>", "type": "preset" }],
  "generate_audio": true,
  "aspect_ratio": "9:16",
  "resolution": "1080p",
  "duration": 15,
  "prompt": "... scene ... Negative: no hangtag, no swing tag, no on-screen text, clean image only."
}
```

The full button-driven version is the `/higgsfield-content-factory` skill.

## Does this matter for Physio and Sole Clinic?

Honestly — not much, which is why I scored it a 2.

This pipeline is built around a *product photo*. You upload a thing, it animates a person
using that thing. A physio clinic doesn't have a product to anchor it; we sell an
assessment, a pair of hands, an outcome. The tool has no obvious hook for "watch someone
get treated."

Where it *could* earn its keep: the small retail side — insoles, orthotic sandals, braces,
any physical SKU we actually sell. For those, this is a fast, cheap way to get scroll-
stopping social creative, in formats already proven in-category, without booking a shoot.
For the core clinical service, it's the wrong tool — and worth saying so plainly rather
than forcing it.
